No shame in talking it out: AFL players support Yarn Safe for Indigenous Round

28/05/2015

AFL stars Hawthorn's Cyril Rioli, Port Adelaide's Chad Wingard and Melbourne's Neville Jetta are backing the message of theheadspaceYarn Safe campaign: if you've got a lot going on - there's no shame in talking it out.

Yarn Safe is the first youth-led national Aboriginal and Torres Strait Islander mental health campaign, telling young people there's no shame in talking about problems affecting their mental health and wellbeing.

 

headspace and the AFL Players' Association have partnered on the campaign to coincide with the 2015 Toyota AFL Indigenous Round this weekend.

The campaign includes a video with the three young men talking about their culture and identity; the pressures they experience; the important relationships in their lives; and how they deal with stress.

Chad Wingard said he was a great supporter of Yarn Safe, which encourages Aboriginal and Torres Strait Islander young people to speak up and ask for help when going through a tough time.

"The stress that you can get, it can really bottle up and take over you and really destroy you as a person if you don't get it out," he said.

"I had some people that I was comfortable talking to and it felt safe. headspace is a great place to go to elevate your problems and get help."

 

headspace CEO Chris Tanti welcomed the player's commitment to the Yarn Safe campaign.

"Two years ago headspace set out on a path that had not been traversed before - to create an engagement campaign that would encourage young Aboriginal and Torres Strait Islander people to seek help when needed," Mr Tanti said.

"Already headspace has seen dramatic increases in the number of young Aboriginal and Torres Strait Islander people walking through our doors, increasing from 7.6 per cent to almost 10 per cent in just a few months.

"headspace has an ongoing commitment to culturally sensitive practice and evaluation and we will continue to ensure that more young Aboriginal and Torres Strait Islander people feel comfortable to talk to us.

"We're thrilled that Cyril, Chad and Neville are not only lending their support to Yarn Safe, but have openly discussed their experiences and feelings.

"The importance of these three young men, who are respected by their communities for their activities both on and off the field, cannot be overstated. They fearlessly face the camera and say there is no shame in talking it out."

The 2015 Toyota AFL Indigenous Round is an opportunity to celebrate the role Aboriginal and Torres Strait Islander players have had in shaping Australian Rules football.

AFL Players' Association CEO Paul Marsh said he is thrilled that a partnership has been formed with headspace to help improve mental health in Aboriginal and Torres Strait Islander young people.

"Player support and mental wellbeing is fundamental to our organisation, so to have three outstanding guys such as Chad, Cyril and Neville passing on the message that there's no shame in talking about their issues, reinforces our daily practice here at AFL Players'," he said.

Mr Tanti thanked the AFL Players' Association and the players for their support of Yarn Safe.

"I am proud headspace has had the opportunity to collaborate with the AFL Players' Association and Chad, Cyril and Neville to take yet another step in this long but vital journey to close the gap between Indigenous and non-Indigenous Australians," he said.

"We are taking the long view on this issue because it needs that focus and we will continue to collaborate with communities across the country to meet their needs."

 

Please note:

headspace and AFL Players' Association want to acknowledge Aboriginal and Torres Strait Islander people as the First Peoples of Australia and pay our respects to their elders past, present and future. We recognise the importance of connection to culture and the players involved in this campaign have identified their cultural and language group as below, this is featured throughout our campaign in recognition of their cultural heritage:

Neville Jetta - Nyoongar; Cyril Rioli - Tiwi; and Chad Wingard - Kaurna & Ngarrindjeri.

About Yarn Safe

Yarn Safe was first launched in 2014 with the help of 12 Aboriginal and Torres Strait Islander young people from across Australia. It aims to improve mental health literacy among Aboriginal and Torres Strait Islander young people and increase the number coming to headspace for problems affecting their mental health and wellbeing.

 

Is there a lot going on? Everyone feels sad, down, stressed or angry at times. It's normal. But it's not okay if you or a friend is feeling like that for long periods of time or if it's stopping you from doing the things you used to.

If you feel like there is too much happening clearing your head can help. If you'd like to talk to someone about what's going on for you, there's a place where you can yarn safe. There is no shame in talking it out.

headspace is your space.

About headspace

The primary focus of headspace is the mental health and wellbeing of young Australians. headspace helps 12 - 25 year olds going through a tough time through a national network of  headspace centres and online and telephone counselling service eheadspace. headspace can help young people with general health, mental health, education and employment and alcohol and other drug services. headspace was established and funded by the Commonwealth Government of Australia in 2006. Visit headspace.org.au to find a headspace centre or access help.

 

About AFL Players' Association

The AFL Players' Association has a 40 year rich history and has entrenched itself as a key stakeholder in the AFL industry. With a 100% uptake of membership, the AFL Players' Association has grown in influence and size to become a respected champion of players and the game.

headspace National Youth Mental Health Foundation Ltd is funded by the Australian Government Department of Health under the Youth Mental Health Initiative Program.
