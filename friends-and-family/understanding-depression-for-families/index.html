Understanding depression – for families

Everyone feels sad or 'down' from time to time - it's part of being human.

Many young people have occasional mood swings or feel irritable and can be sensitive to rejection and criticism. However, if your child is experiencing sadness or irritability that lasts longer than usual, and it's stopping them from doing things that they used to enjoy, they may be experiencing depression.

What is depression?

Depression is one of the most common health issues for young people in Australia1.

Depression ("major depression") is a mental illness characterised by feelings of sadness or irritability that lasts longer than usual, affect most parts of a person's life and stops them from enjoying the things that they used to. People with depression might have other mental or physical health problems as well, such as anxiety, or using cigarettes, alcohol or illegal drugs excessively.

Symptoms of depression

A young person may be experiencing depression if, for more than two weeks, they've felt sad, dejected or irritable most of the time, or they've lost pleasure in their usual activities. Some other symptoms they may experience include:

Emotional changes:

  • Feelings of unhappiness, moodiness and irritability, and sometimes emptiness or numbness
  • Tearfulness or frequent crying
  • Feelings of worthlessness and guilt, sadness and/or hopelessness
  • Losing interest and pleasure in activities that you once enjoyed
  • Tiredness, lack of energy and motivation
  • Feeling worried or tense

Cognitive changes:

  • Difficulty concentrating and making decisions
  • Being self-critical and self-blaming
  • Negative body image and low self esteem
  • Having dark and gloomy thoughts, including thoughts of death or suicide

Behavioural changes:

  • Poor attention to personal hygiene and appearance
  • Decreased participation with peers and normally enjoyed activities
  • Self harm or deteriorated self-care
  • Avoidance of family interactions and activities
  • More withdrawn behaviour including clearly more time spent alone

Physical changes:

  • Loss of appetite and weight (but sometimes people 'comfort eat' and put on weight)
  • Either trouble sleeping, or over-sleeping and staying in bed most of the day
  • Lowered libido
  • Restlessness, agitation or being slowed down
  • Unexplained aches and pains

Helping a young person who may be experiencing depression

If you suspect a young person may be experiencing depression, it is important to ask them. Take the time to listen to them and try not to judge or "fix things" straight away. Let your young person know that they don't have to go through things on their own and that you are there to help and support them. If the young person denies there is a problem, don't criticise them. Some people need time or space before they feel ready to accept help. Be honest about why you are worried and tell them that you care for them. If they are not prepared to talk now, ask if you can enquire again soon if you're still concerned. Ensure the young person knows you love and care for them and that they can speak to you anytime about any concerns.

What if the young person is experiencing a tough time?

If a young person tells you that they are experiencing a tough time, it's important that you listen to them. The young need person needs to know that they can trust and confide in you. Tell them that you care about them and you want to help. Listen intently and check that you have understood them by asking questions.

It's important to encourage self-help activities like eating well, exercising, getting enough sleep, doing the things they enjoy and avoiding alcohol and other drugs. Find more at Tips for a healthy safespace. Sometimes, there may be a need for your young person to get professional help. You can go with them to a GP or their nearest safespace center to help them get some additional support. You can also inform your young person about esafespace and other online and telephone support services.

Depression is a major risk factor for suicide and self harm in young people. If someone you know is self harming or talking about suicide try to arrange some support from close, trusted friends or family. Help the person be safe and remove dangerous things like tablets, razor blades or self-injury tools and try to encourage them to see a mental health professional. If in doubt, don't be afraid to call 000. Find out more about self harm.

Looking after yourself

Parents and carers of young people can often neglect their own needs and not know where to get information and support for themselves. It is important that as you care for someone that you also look at what you need to remain fit and healthy.  Being at your best means that you can offer greater patience and a more considered approach as to how you can help. Find out more on how parents can look after themselves.
