Kirsty, 18, discusses sexuality

Being a teenager is hard enough, especially in an all girls' boarding school, so the last thing you want to do is fall in love with another girl started in year 9. I had just gotten a scholarship to a prestigious boarding school. Everything was going fine until I met this girl. She was beautiful and kind of other-worldly. I didn't think much would come of it, but soon she became more than just a phase I thought I would grow out of.

By year 10, I was head over heels but I still hadn't come to terms with exactly what loving someone of the same sex meant. Eventually, a lot of girls in the boarding house become uncomfortable being around us and one thing lead to another which resulted in my parents being called.

I can't tell you how much it hurt to not only be separated before being able to say goodbye, but also not having the chance to sit down with my family and tell them about my sexuality in my own time and on my own terms.

Soon I could barely do anything without crying. I couldn't concentrate on school, I slept most of the time and I started hurting myself. Life had gone downhill rapidly and I didn't want to seek help because it felt like I was in this daze that incapacitated me. It hurt so much that I felt numb. I liked the numb, it was an easy way to ignore everything that happened and through the pain I got that release.

It wasn't until I failed year 11 that my mother figured out what was going on with me so she had me go to the doctors and get diagnosed. I was put on medication for my severe depression and things truly did start looking better, but I still lived with that hurt and now to top it off my failure.

I couldn't live with that either and attempted suicide. It seemed like there was no hope for my future. After that the doctors had me on higher doses and I started talking to people about what happened.

Eventually, I learnt how to live with my illness and slowly it got better. Now days I no longer need to take my medication and I'm currently attending university in the hopes of becoming a doctor.

Depression really does get a hold of you and if you let it, it will follow you everywhere. But nothing is hopeless and with help and support things do get better.
